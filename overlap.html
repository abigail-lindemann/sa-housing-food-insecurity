The Signature Finding

The quadrant map is the core finding of this project. Every other analysis — the housing cost burden map, the grocery distance map — is a single dimension. This map crosses them.

The darkest red block groups are the places where the math is hardest: families paying more than half their income on rent, in neighborhoods where the nearest supermarket requires a car they may not have. These are not adjacent problems — they're the same families, in the same block groups, at the same time.

The geographic pattern matters too. Dual insecurity tends not to be randomly distributed — it clusters, often in a band connecting the South Side, East Side, and parts of the West Side that have seen decades of disinvestment. That's the shape of the problem.

Service Gaps in Dual-Insecurity Zones

Toggle on the service layer above to see food banks, pantries, and WIC clinics overlaid on the quadrant map. The pattern that emerges in many cities — and may emerge here — is that service infrastructure is thinner in exactly the places where need is highest.

Where dual-insecurity block groups fall outside a reasonable distance of a food bank or WIC clinic, those are the coverage gaps: measurable, mappable, addressable with targeted investment.

What the Data Can and Can't Say

This analysis measures potential access — distance from the center of a block group to the nearest store. It doesn't capture car ownership, public transit access, store hours, or store quality. A grocery store within a mile is not the same thing as food security.

The housing insecurity score is similarly a proxy — it captures the structural conditions of cost burden and overcrowding, not the lived experience of housing instability. Both measures are starting points for deeper investigation, not final answers.

Methodology note — quadrant classification

Each block group is classified into one of four quadrants based on two thresholds: a housing insecurity score above 0.5 (on a 0–1 normalized scale) is considered "high housing insecurity"; a nearest grocery store distance above 1.0 miles is considered "high food access concern." These thresholds are documented choices, not fixed standards — they can be adjusted in notebook 03_overlap_analysis.ipynb to explore sensitivity. The housing insecurity score is computed at the block group level; grocery distance is computed from block group centroids to the nearest SNAP-authorized full-service grocery store. See individual methodology notes on the housing and food pages for full details.
