Two Definitions of a Food Desert

The USDA Food Desert Atlas uses a 1-mile urban threshold — if a meaningful share of a low-income tract's residents live more than a mile from a supermarket, the entire tract qualifies. The 0.5-mile threshold is stricter: roughly a 10-minute walk, the distance at which access is genuinely walkable.

This analysis shows both simultaneously. The gap between the two thresholds reveals the "car-dependent access zone" — tracts where a grocery store exists within a mile but is not within comfortable walking distance. For residents without cars, that mile might as well be ten.

The SNAP retailer data captures the most current picture: updated monthly from USDA FNS, it reflects store openings and closures as they happen. The USDA Food Desert Atlas, by contrast, is updated every few years — so the two sources occasionally disagree in rapidly changing neighborhoods.

SNAP & WIC as Outcome Proxies

SNAP (Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program) participation rates are included as an outcome proxy for food insecurity alongside spatial access metrics. High SNAP participation in a tract indicates both elevated food need and the presence of people already engaged with federal nutrition support — a different kind of signal than distance alone.

WIC (Women, Infants, and Children) clinic locations serve a similar purpose: high WIC participation in a neighborhood is a leading indicator of food insecurity among young families. Clinic locations are shown on the map as points; their presence (or absence) near high-distance tracts highlights service coverage gaps.

Food Banks & Pantries

The San Antonio Food Bank operates one of the largest food distribution networks in the country, supplemented by dozens of community pantries served through 211 San Antonio. Food bank and pantry locations are shown as points on the map — toggle them on to see how well they cover the highest-distance tracts.

Where food bank locations are sparse in high-distance tracts, those are the coverage gaps: places where both the market and the safety net are thin. The combined view (link below) crosses these gaps with housing insecurity data to identify the most vulnerable block groups.

Methodology note — food access distance analysis

Distance from each block group centroid to the nearest SNAP-authorized full-service grocery store (supermarket, large, medium, or small grocery) was computed using geodesic distance in the analysis notebook 02_food_access.ipynb. Store locations are sourced from the USDA FNS SNAP retailer list, pulled monthly via the USDA ArcGIS open data endpoint. Only stores classified as Supermarket, Large Grocery Store, Medium Grocery Store, or Small Grocery Store are included; convenience stores, specialty markets, and pharmacies are excluded.

The USDA Food Desert Atlas (2019 edition) provides tract-level food desert designations (LILATracts_1And10 and LILATracts_halfAnd10) and is joined to census tracts by FIPS code. Because the Atlas uses 2015 store data, there may be discrepancies with the current SNAP retailer list in neighborhoods where stores have opened or closed.
