More than half of Bexar County's neighborhoods have no full-service grocery store within a mile — the threshold the USDA uses to define a food desert in urban areas. For the 191 block groups within half a mile of a store, grocery access is walkable. For the 544 beyond a mile, getting food requires a car, a bus ride, or a long walk.

What the map shows

Block groups are shaded by distance to the nearest SNAP-authorized full-service grocery store. Darker green indicates worse access. Grocery store locations are shown as markers. Click any block group to see its distance to the nearest store, food bank, and WIC clinic.

The USDA standard vs. our measure

The USDA Food Desert Atlas designates 222 Bexar County block groups as food deserts — areas that are both low-income and low-access. Our distance-based measure identifies 544 block groups beyond the 1-mile threshold, including higher-income areas where residents still face long distances to grocery stores. The two measures together capture different dimensions of the same problem.

Food banks and WIC clinics

San Antonio's 10 food bank and pantry locations and 15 WIC clinics are unevenly distributed across the county. The average block group is 4 miles from a food bank and 3.3 miles from a WIC clinic. In dual-insecurity neighborhoods — those with both high housing cost burden and poor grocery access — the average distance to a food bank rises to 4.7 miles.

Data notes

Grocery store locations are drawn from the USDA SNAP-authorized retailer list, filtered to full-service stores (supermarkets and large, medium, and small grocery stores). Convenience stores, specialty food stores, and pharmacies are excluded. Food bank and WIC locations are maintained as manually curated static files, updated annually.
