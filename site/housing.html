The Cost Burden Picture

Housing experts define "cost-burdened" as spending more than 30 percent of household income on housing costs. In Bexar County, that threshold catches a large share of renters — disproportionately in the South, East, and West sides of the city, where incomes are lower and older housing stock makes ownership less accessible.

Severe cost burden — more than 50 percent of income on housing — is the sharper edge. Households in this category have little left for food, transportation, healthcare, or emergencies. The map shows where these households cluster.

Overcrowding (more than one person per room) captures a different dimension of housing stress: families doubling up because they cannot afford separate units. It is concentrated in block groups with the lowest median incomes.

Income & Demographics

Housing insecurity scores track closely with median household income across block groups. Below $35,000 annual income, the share of cost-burdened renters rises sharply — a pattern visible across the South Side, the East Side, and neighborhoods near downtown where gentrification has pushed rents faster than incomes have grown.

Hispanic or Latino households represent the majority of residents in the highest-burden block groups, consistent with broader patterns of housing inequity in Texas cities. The income–insecurity relationship holds even within demographic groups, suggesting the mechanism is income-level, not solely demographic.

Service Coverage Gap

High housing insecurity scores don't automatically mean proximity to housing assistance resources. The combined view (see link below) maps service coverage gaps — block groups where insecurity is high but the nearest housing assistance provider or food bank is more than a mile away.

These gap zones represent the planning challenge: neighborhoods where need is measurable and visible, but service infrastructure hasn't caught up.

Methodology note — housing insecurity score

The housing insecurity score for each block group is computed as a normalized (0–1) average of three ACS variables: renter cost burden rate (share of renters paying >30% of income on housing, from Table B25070), severe cost burden rate (share paying >50%, also B25070), and overcrowding rate (share of units with more than 1.0 persons per room, from Table B25014). Each variable is independently normalized to its county-wide min–max range before averaging. Block groups with fewer than 50 renter-occupied units are flagged as low-reliability estimates.

Eviction data was evaluated as a fourth indicator but excluded from this version. Eviction Lab no longer provides public bulk downloads at the census tract level; a formal data request is required. A future update will incorporate Bexar County eviction filings from Texas Housers (texashousers.org), which tracks these annually via Public Information Act requests. See the README for details.
