155 block groups in Bexar County — 13.6 percent of all neighborhoods — show high insecurity on both housing and food dimensions simultaneously. These are the areas where families face the highest combined pressure: paying more than they can afford for housing while also living far from a grocery store.

What the map shows

Each block group is classified into one of four quadrants based on its housing insecurity score (top 25% = high) and food access score (beyond 1 mile or USDA food desert = high). Purple indicates dual insecurity — the most vulnerable neighborhoods. Click any block group to see its full profile.

The dual-insecurity neighborhoods

The 155 dual-insecurity block groups are not randomly distributed. They concentrate on the South and West sides of San Antonio, in areas with median household incomes below $35,000 and Hispanic population shares above 80 percent. These neighborhoods face compounding disadvantages: high housing costs relative to income, and limited access to affordable food.

Service gaps

Of the 155 dual-insecurity block groups, 72 are also more than 3 miles from both a food bank and a WIC clinic. These are the neighborhoods with the least access to the support systems designed to help. The average dual-insecurity block group is 4.7 miles from a food bank — nearly a mile further than the county average.

High food insecurity only

The 461 block groups with high food insecurity but lower housing stress are largely in the outer suburbs and rapidly developing areas where new housing has outpaced grocery infrastructure. These areas have relatively higher incomes but still face significant distance barriers to fresh food.
